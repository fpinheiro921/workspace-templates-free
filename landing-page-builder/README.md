# Landing Page Academy — High-Converting Landing Page System

> **Source:** Erik Kennedy — Landing Page Academy
> **Use:** Every time you build or redesign a landing page, copy this template and work through it step by step.

---

## Overview

A landing page is a webpage that sells a product and drives visitors to a specific action (a "conversion"). It's a blind sales pitch where the customer can walk away at any point with zero social pressure — and can't ask questions.

**Key insight:** Doubling conversions more than doubles profits. Small copy changes to one webpage can affect as much as giant product changes.

```
Profit = (Traffic × Conversion Rate × LCV) − Expenses
```

If conversion rate doubles and LCV goes up even slightly → profit more than doubles.

## Phase 1: Copywriting Process

### Step 1: Determine Goals

**Primary conversion** (pick one):
- Download / Sign up / Buy / Begin free trial / Subscribe to email list / Schedule a call

**Secondary objectives:**
- Purchase secondary products
- Read related content (guides, blog posts)
- View/follow social media
- Share on social media

**Common objections** (write out yours specifically):
1. "I don't know what it does"
2. "I don't know why I'd need it"
3. "I don't believe it does what it claims to do"
4. "I don't believe it will help people like me"
5. "I don't know if it'll work with my current system/setup"
6. "My current system/setup is good enough"
7. "It'll take too much time"
8. "It'll take too much effort"
9. "It's too expensive"
10. "I'll buy it later"

**Balancing multiple conversions:**
- Which is currently most profitable?
- Is one the direction the business wants to move in?
- Which allows cross-selling / highest CLV?
- Can you split the ATF into two sections?
- Can you separate into multiple LPs?

### Step 2: Headline & Subhead

**The Headline Formula:**
The (a) most succinct statement of (b) your greatest value for (c) the greatest number of your customers.

**The Self-Selection Principle:**
Every plausible customer, upon reading the headline, should know this site offers something of value for them.

**A good headline:**
- Describes the biggest value of your product/service
- Is maximally succinct
- Makes the visitor want to scroll down (a hook)
- Is accurate and honest
- Says something your competitors could not
- Addresses the customer's biggest objection
- Signals to all potential customers there's value for them

**A good headline is NOT:**
- Vague ("Supercharge your marketing")
- Jargony ("The ultimate project management solution")
- What the business cares about, but the user doesn't ("The world's most innovative home-rental platform")
- Clever at the expense of clarity ("Purpose-Powered Productivity")
- Forces the user to wait (e.g. carousel)

**Headline brainstorming templates:**
- The all-in-one `<noun>` for `<action>`
- The `<superlative>` `<noun>` for `<action>`
- How `<customer segment>` `<does something they want to do>`
- `<Achieve benefit>` even if `<common objection>`
- `<Benefit>`. No `<common objection>`.
- `<Benefit>` for less than `<reasonable sounding cost>`
- `<simple description of product>`. `<how you describe it when explaining/selling in-person>`

**Subhead formula:** How you'll provide the value in the headline.
- Make the value visible ("See top jobs in your area")
- Make the value feel near ("Get started in <60 seconds")
- Address objections ("Join 10k+ freelancers")
- Make me want to click it ("Send me the goods!")
- Don't make me dread clicking it ("Get emails" ❌)

**Brainstorming rules:** Quantity > quality. No bad ideas. Build on each other's ideas.

### Step 3: Call-to-Action

- Expose the value (make it visible, close-at-hand)
- Address objections
- Don't make me dread clicking it

**Good CTAs:** "Start Your 14-Day Free Trial" / "See Top Jobs In Your Area" / "Get Started in <60 seconds" / "Join 10k+ Freelancers"
**Bad CTAs:** "Subscribe" / "Join" / "Sign Up" / "Get Emails"

### Step 4: Conversion-Oriented Imagery

**Brand imagery** shows the overall vibe.
**Conversion-oriented imagery** shows the benefit.

**What to show:**
- What can you do/make with the product?
- What is life like with the product?
- What problem(s) does the product solve?
- Physical product in situ
- A demo
- A happy customer
- A before/after
- A stat or data viz

**Formats:** Photo / Video or video collage / Illustration / Data viz / Screenshot

### Step 5: Maximizing Conversions Above the Fold

Include all 5 above the fold:
1. Headline & subhead with primary benefit
2. Bullet points with secondary benefits/features
3. CTA with first step
4. Social proof
5. Conversion-oriented imagery

### Step 6: Outlining the Landing Page

**8-slide structure:**
1. Above-the-fold
2. Benefit
3. Feature
4. Teach by example
5. Address objections
6. Secondary calls-to-action
7. Social proof
8. Final call-to-action

**Outlining questions:**
- What is the most remarkable thing about `<X>`?
- What keywords and phrases help people understand and want `<X>` when you explain it in person?
- If you sell `<X>` in person, what's the first objection they give / question they ask?
- When customers describe what they love about `<X>`, what words do you hear often?
- Who needs `<X>`?
- Benefits: How does `<X>` help people win money / time / social status / skills / confidence / belonging / the ability to be the person they want to be?
- Features: What features do customers use/love the most?
- Teach by Example: What examples would make your value more clear?
- Addressing objections: How do customers know `<X>` is easy / fast / risk-free / worth it / worth buying now?
- Secondary Conversions: What other actions do you want to encourage?
- Social Proof: Past customers / name-brand customers / reviews / testimonials / awards / years in business?
- Final CTA: Summarize and push for the sale

**Writing good subheads:**
- The subheads alone should tell 90% of the story
- Together, subheads should cover all major benefits and features
- Don't be clever at the expense of clarity
- Don't talk about features at the expense of explaining benefits

### Step 7: Principles of Great Copywriting

1. **Remove as much text as possible**
2. **Write the strongest true statement that you can** — "Write one true sentence" (Hemingway)
3. **What sells in-person sells online**
4. **It's not about you; it's about what's in it for them**
5. **Cleverness is the enemy; clarity is the antidote**
6. **Write like someone you'd want to buy from** — clear, confident, honest, & funny
7. **Write for the person on the fence**
8. **Write melodically** — vary sentence length, rhythm
9. **Write for scannability** — subheads, bullets, short paragraphs
10. **Write for hookiness** — tell a story that demands resolution, make a bold claim, ask a question, introduce a new term, make a prediction, write a listicle
11. **Get them nodding in agreement about the problem, then show the solution**

**The "as soon as" framework:**
A visitor is ready to convert as soon as:
1. They understand they have the problem you solve
2. They understand the value of the product
3. They have zero remaining objections

### Step 8: Teach by Example

Show, don't tell. Examples:
- Range of services you provide
- Range of products you sell
- Range of situations you're valuable in
- Customer case studies / success stories
- Compelling photos/visuals that help customers understand & want to buy
- Steps in your typical process

### Step 9: Social Proof

**7 types of social proof:**
1. Testimonials
2. Number of customers
3. Brand-name customers
4. Press
5. Reviews (avg, count, 5-star)
6. Awards
7. Company age

**Getting testimonials:**
- Comb past comments
- Ask known fans
- Ask at peak moment
- Ask after conversion ("one month after purchase")
- Ask after engagement metric ("user logs in weekly for 4 weeks")

**Testimonial outreach email template:**
```
Hey [Name],
One thing we're doing at [company] is redesigning our landing page,
and we'd love to feature testimonials from happy customers.

Zero pressure, but if you're interested, even a sentence on why
you appreciate [product] or what it's helped with would be super
useful to future customers.

If not, no worries – we won't bug you about this again :)

Best,
[Your Name]
```

**Keys:** Tell them where their words might be used / No one sends only 1 sentence — make it sound easy / Zero pressure / Remind them this will be useful to others

**Editing testimonials:**
- Superlative angle ("the best", "the greatest", "the quickest")
- Transformative angle ("before, X; now, Y")
- Balance between hype and content
- Every word must carry weight
- Leave viewers mystified that a product could be this good

**Placement:** Above the fold / In their own social proof slides / Integrated with feature/benefit/objection slides

**Don't:** Damn with faint praise / Require waiting to see them (carousels)

## Phase 2: Visual Design Process

### Step 1: Determine Brand

Brand = how you want users to perceive you. It's the link between coherent visual design and individual choices.

**Brand adjectives (pick 3-5):** Adventurous, Artsy, Authoritative, Balanced, Beautiful, Bold, Caring, Casual, Cheerful, Clean, Confident, Cool, Creative, Cutting-Edge, Earthy, Elegant, Essential, Friendly, Futuristic, Geeky, Human, Humble, Inviting, Luxury, Minimalist, Modern, Natural, Professional, Quirky, Simple, Sleek, Sophisticated, Techie, Trustworthy, etc.

**Brand reflection questions:**
- Who are you?
- What do you have to say?
- Why should people listen to you?
- What do you believe that all your competitors disagree with?
- What values do you hold so strongly, you'd lose money rather than violate them?
- What aren't you? What values define everything you stand against?
- What is the most likely way people will misinterpret you?

## Phase 3: Testing

### The 10-Second Landing Page Test

**When to use:** Existing LP redesign / In-progress design / Small high-impact tweaks

**Process:**
1. Write down the main points your site needs to communicate
2. Find participants (plausible customers, not current customers)
3. Conduct the test: show the page for 10 seconds → close eyes → describe what they remember
4. Improve your site based on findings

### User Testing

**5-step process:**
1. Write the testing script
2. Find participants (from network, users' network, product users)
3. Screen participants (demographic + behavioral criteria)
4. Perform the test
5. Create a report

**The grand challenge:** Finding plausible customers who are not yet actual customers.

**Screening criteria examples:**
- "Do you know what 'direct primary care' refers to?"
- "Are you a developer?"
- "How often do you compost?"
- Donated at least $300 in the past year / Have never donated to GiveWell

**Test script structure:**
- Introduction (set expectations, this is a test of the website not them, think out loud, can be recorded?)
- Tasks (realistic scenario → study page 10 sec → close eyes → recall → browse → decide use or leave)
- Conclusion (What was this page about? What are you confused about? Would you purchase? What's your biggest hesitation?)

**Best practices during test:**
- Keep them thinking out loud ("What are you thinking right now?")
- Use mirroring ("You don't get it?")
- Ask clarifying questions ("What's hard to read?")
- Ask what they'd do if you weren't there
- If confused by results, ask what they expected to happen

## Process Mindset: Key Principles

1. **Clarity > obscurity**
2. **Making the value explicit > implicit**
3. **Repetition of CTAs > only one CTA**
4. **Recommending one choice > presenting equal options**
5. **Removing the distracting, optional, and irrelevant > including it**

### ChatGPT Canvas Copy Editing Workflow (Jon Benson Method)

50-80% of copywriting is editing. ChatGPT Canvas (visual document editor) makes this dramatically faster.

**Step 1: Critique First, Then Edit**
```
Prompt: I'm going to share landing page copy with you.
FIRST, give me inline comments and critique — do NOT edit yet.
Act as Gary Halbert reviewing this copy. What would he say?
Focus on: weak headlines, missing tension, unclear value,
buried CTAs, and any violation of the 11 commandments.

[Paste copy]
```
**Why this works:** Asking for critique FIRST produces dramatically better edits than direct "edit this" prompts. The AI identifies problems with context before making changes.

**Step 2: Apply Edits Based on Critique**
```
Prompt: Now apply the improvements you identified.
Keep the voice consistent. Don't change sections that were fine.
Make the copy more: specific, tension-driven, melodically varied.
```

**Step 3: Burstiness Polish (Passes AI Detection)**
```
Prompt: Increase the burstiness of this copy by 10x.
Vary sentence length dramatically — some 3 words, some 20.
This makes it read as human-written.
```
**Why this works:** "Burstiness" = sentence length variation. AI-generated content has uniform sentence lengths. Increasing burstiness makes it pass human detection and reads more naturally.

**Step 4: Litotes + Bombastic Contrast**
```
Prompt: Use extreme litotes contrasted with extreme bombastic statements.
Example: "It's not magic. It's not revolutionary. It will, however, 
completely transform how you write landing pages forever."
```

**Step 5: Reading Level Adjustment**
```
Prompt: Adjust the reading level to 7th grade.
Good copy is simple. Complex language kills conversions.
```

**Step 6: Final Polish**
```
Prompt: One-click polish pass. Remove all emojis.
Fix formatting. Ensure consistent voice throughout.
```
