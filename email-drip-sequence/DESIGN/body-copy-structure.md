# Body Copy Structure

> The 8-step framework for writing a highly readable deep dive email — optimized for ~800 words and a 3–4 minute read.

---

## Why Structure Matters

Your emails need to do two things: create the **sensation of learning** and **entertain**. Structure creates the sensation of learning. Voice creates entertainment. Structure is teachable and can be applied immediately. Voice takes ~25,000 words of writing to develop.

This 8-step framework produces consistently readable emails regardless of topic. Apply it in order. Repeat steps 3–6 for each subtopic within a single email.

---

## The 8 Steps

### Step 1 — Introduce the Idea with a Hook

**Goal:** Stop the scroll. Make the reader want to continue.

**Options:**
- A short personal story (honest, slightly embarrassing is good)
- A reframing of a problem the reader didn't know they had
- A surprising claim or counterintuitive statement
- A quote that reframes the topic

**Length:** Max 4 sentences. This is not the time to explain — it's the time to intrigue.

**Examples:**
> "A few days after my final interview with a startup, the hiring manager called with an offer. Then he said, 'We can also offer you 35,000 options at a $2 strike price.' Uh..."

> "I built my first email course in 2019 and it flopped completely. Here's the one thing I got wrong that nobody talks about."

---

### Step 2 — Set Expectations Immediately

**Goal:** Tell the reader what they're getting, how long it'll take, and where it leads.

**Why:** Our brains crave duration, path, and outcome. Without this, readers feel unmoored and stop reading.

**What to include:**
- Read time (already in header, but reference it)
- What they'll understand by the end
- Where this fits in the overall course

**Example:**
> "In this email, we're going to cover [topic]. By the end, you'll know [specific outcome]. This is part 2 of 4 in this chapter — after this, we move into [what's next]."

---

### Step 3 — Define Concepts

**Goal:** Make sure the reader has the vocabulary to understand what's coming.

**Rule:** Never use a term you haven't defined. Define before you dive — don't assume shared vocabulary.

**Format:** Simple, plain definitions. Bold the term. One sentence per concept.

**Example:**
> "The **strike price** is the pre-determined price at which you can purchase company stock. The **vesting schedule** is the timeline over which increments of options become available to exercise."

*Note: Repeat steps 3–6 for each subtopic if covering multiple ideas in one email.*

---

### Step 4 — Provide Context

**Goal:** Answer "WHY is this subtopic important?" — the big-picture "so what."

**Distinction from Step 5:**
- Context = big picture (why this matters in general)
- Relevance = right now (what happens if the reader doesn't act)

**Example:**
> "Understanding vesting schedules matters because most startup employees never fully vest their options — they leave before the cliff. That's money they earned but never received."

---

### Step 5 — Demonstrate Relevance

**Goal:** Make the reader feel the personal stakes. What happens RIGHT NOW if they don't take action? What's the cost of the status quo?

**Technique:** Specific numbers make relevance land. Vague consequences don't.

**Example:**
> "If I had 35,000 options at a $2 strike price and the company IPO'd at $20, not exercising those options would mean leaving $630,000 on the table — before taxes."

---

### Step 6 — Give Great Examples

**Goal:** Lock in understanding with something tangible. The brain treats vivid examples as near-real experience.

**Rules:**
- Examples should align to the reader's actual life — not abstract scenarios
- Use images when the concept is visual
- If you have a real case, use it over a hypothetical

**Principle:** "Context is king, examples are queen." Concepts without examples leave the reader able to repeat words without understanding the idea.

---

### Step 7 — Summarize Key Takeaways

**Goal:** Recreate the sensation of learning. Put the reader in the position of thinking "oh yeah, I did just learn all that."

**Format:** 2–4 bullet points. Plain language. Mirror the Key Takeaways box at the top.

**Why it works:** The summary doesn't feel repetitive — it feels like consolidation. The brain registers it as a second learning moment.

**Example:**
> "To recap what we covered:
> - The strike price is fixed at grant; the value comes from the stock price rising above it
> - Vesting schedules determine when you can actually access your options
> - Not exercising before expiration means losing them entirely"

---

### Step 8 — Foreshadow What's Coming

**Goal:** Give the reader a reason to come back. You're the trail guide — give them a sense of what's around the next curve.

**Format:** 1–2 sentences. Make the next email feel like a natural continuation, not a separate thing.

**Example:**
> "In the next email, we're going deep on the tax implications of exercising options — specifically when to exercise early vs. waiting for the IPO, and why timing this wrong can cost you more than you made."

---

## Readability Tactics

| Tactic | Application |
|--------|------------|
| **Short paragraphs** | 2–3 sentences max; never 800-word blocks |
| **Horizontal rules** | Break the page between major sections |
| **Bold for key terms** | Defines on first use; scannable on re-read |
| **Q&A format** | Use bolded questions as subheadings for conversational feel |
| **Whitespace** | More space = easier reading; don't compress |

---

## Voice Tactics

**Q&A format** — Anticipate reader questions and answer them in sequence. Forces you to prioritize and creates dialogue.

**Metaphor** — Use to deepen understanding, not just restate. "Financial statements are report cards — but when you're public, you can't hide them from your mom."

**Personal story** — Share real experience. The emotion has to come from a real place. Even a brief story adds warmth.

**Humor** — Find it, don't add it. Strip jokes that feel forced. Keep only what would make someone audibly laugh. Being selective makes humor more effective, not less.

---

## The Full 8-Step at a Glance

```
1. Hook               — Grab attention in 4 sentences
2. Set expectations   — Duration, path, outcome
3. Define concepts    — Vocabulary before you use it
4. Provide context    — Why this matters (big picture)
5. Demonstrate relevance — Cost of status quo (right now)
6. Give examples      — Tangible, real, specific
7. Summarize          — Recreate the learning sensation
8. Foreshadow         — One reason to come back

Repeat steps 3–6 for each subtopic.
```
