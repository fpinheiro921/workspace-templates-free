# Email Archetypes

> The 7 structural email types that make up a complete EBC — each with a distinct goal, format, and placement rule.

---

## How Archetypes Work Together

The longer your course, the more "structural support" you need — mechanisms that remind the reader where they are, where they're going, and why it matters. Structural support means chapter overviews, quizzes, action items, and recaps — all the stuff that tells the brain it's making progress.

**Rule:** If your course is < 15 emails, you can skip Chapter Overviews and Quizzes. If it's ≥ 15 emails, you need both.

---

## The 7 Archetypes

### 1. Welcome Email

**Goal:** Land in the primary inbox. Get the reader to take one action that "starts" the course.

**Format:** Plain text only — no logo, no images, no design. Plain text maximizes deliverability.

**Key moves:**
- Acknowledge they signed up
- Set the tone for what's coming
- Ask them to move you to primary inbox (Gmail tabs issue)
- Include one action: click to start the course

**Psychological principle:** The action of starting puts skin in the game. It creates commitment and increases completion.

**Template opening:**
> "Hey [first name], Welcome! Really glad you're here. Before we dive in, I need one quick favor from you..."

---

### 2. Chapter Overview

**Goal:** Map the upcoming content — tell them what they'll learn, where it fits in the grand scheme, and why it matters.

**Format:** Branded (logo + header image that identifies "Chapter Overview" type)

**Structure:**
- What this chapter covers (visual table of contents)
- Why this chapter matters (relevance to their goal)
- What they'll be able to do by the end

**When to use:** Start of every new chapter in a multi-chapter course. Skip in a < 15-email single-chapter course.

**Subject line:** `[Chapter 2] What We're Covering This Week`

---

### 3. Deep Dive

**Goal:** Explore a single topic in depth. The workhorse email of the course.

**Format:** Branded, with header image that identifies the email number and subtopic.

**Structure:** Use the 8-step body copy framework → see `body-copy-structure.md`

**Key principle:** Hook → Set expectations → Repeat steps 3–6 for each subtopic → Summarize → Foreshadow

**Length target:** ~800 words (~3–4 min read time)

**Subject line:** `[2.1] The specific subtopic title`

---

### 4. Quiz

**Goal:** Reinforce knowledge, not test it. Trigger the sensation of having learned something.

**Format:** Interactive (Tally.so or Typeform, linked from the email)

**Rules:**
- 3–4 questions max
- Multiple choice only (allows one clearly correct answer)
- Use "logic jumps" to reinforce the right answer regardless of whether they got it right or wrong
- Use metaphors in questions — new associations deepen understanding
- Quizzes should reinforce, never punish

**Example quiz question:**
> "In what way is consistent content creation most similar to going to the gym?"
> A) It requires expensive equipment
> B) Doing it consistently over time produces great results ← correct
> C) You need a trainer to get started
> D) Results are immediate if you push hard

**Subject line:** `[2.2] Quick check-in before we go deeper`

---

### 5. Action Item

**Goal:** Get the reader to DO something with what they just learned. "Doing" makes learning sticky.

**Format:** Branded, but simpler than a Deep Dive — focused on the one task.

**Rules:**
- ONE action only — never two
- Remove all roadblocks: step-by-step instructions, video walkthroughs if needed, time estimate
- Do the action item yourself before asking students to
- Be upfront about the time commitment

**CTA structure:**
> "Your assignment: [specific task]. This should take about [X minutes]. Here's exactly how to do it: [steps]. Don't move forward until you've done this — it'll make the next section much more useful."

**Subject line:** `[2.3] Action item — [what they're doing]`

---

### 6. Ask (Reviews / Feedback / Surveys)

**Goal:** Get information from the reader — feedback, testimonials, or survey data.

**Format:** Usually embeds a short Tally/Typeform form or asks for a direct reply.

**When to use and what to ask:**

| Course Stage | What to Ask |
|-------------|-------------|
| Early (first 25%) | Clarity and satisfaction: "What was hard to understand? Are you getting what you expected?" |
| Middle | "What's the most valuable thing you've learned so far?" |
| Near end | Social proof: "Would you recommend this course? What would you say?" |

**Never:** Ask for a review at the end of a long revision thread or when they're frustrated. Timing is everything — ask immediately after a positive moment.

**Subject line:** `Quick favor — 2 minutes`

---

### 7. Call to Action (Upsell / Close)

**Goal:** Convert course completers into buyers of your next offer. Should feel like the logical next step — not a sales pitch.

**Format:** Branded, but simpler. Let the content do the work.

**Structure:**
1. Show them how far they've come (recap what they now know/can do)
2. Name the gap: what they still need to go further
3. Introduce the next offer as the solution to that gap
4. State deliverables, price, and timeline clearly
5. One CTA — not three options

**Key principle:** If the EBC was intentionally designed to lead here, the CTA won't feel like selling. It'll feel like completing a thought.

**Subject line:** `You finished. Here's what's next.`

## Email Structure Components (10-Part Anatomy)

Every non-welcome email shares this anatomy:

| # | Component | Purpose |
|---|-----------|---------|
| 1 | Subject line | Short, snappy; includes course position |
| 2 | Pre-header | First text visible in preview pane before opening |
| 3 | Logo | Brand identity; in every email after welcome |
| 4 | Read time | Word count ÷ 250; sets expectations |
| 5 | Header image | Visual cue of email type + subject |
| 6 | Key Takeaways | 3 bullets; plain language; upfront |
| 7 | Body copy | The actual learning content |
| 8 | Fast Forward | Link to trigger next email immediately (self-pacing) |
| 9 | Further Learning | External links — bottom only; never mid-email |
| 10 | Progress Tracker | Visual showing where they are + what's coming |

**Fast Forward:** A link automation that lets engaged readers get the next email immediately rather than waiting for the scheduled send. Build it into every email.
