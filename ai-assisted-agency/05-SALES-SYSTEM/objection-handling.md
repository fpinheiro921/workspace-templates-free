# Objection Handling

## The Frame

Objections are not rejections. They are requests for more information or signals that you skipped a step in the sales process.

**Rule:** Never argue with an objection. Acknowledge it, unpack it, then reframe.

---

## The 3-Step Response Pattern

```
1. ACKNOWLEDGE  → Validate their concern (not agree with it)
2. CLARIFY      → Find out if it's real or a surface objection
3. REFRAME      → Address the actual issue
```

---

## "It's too expensive"

**What they usually mean:** The ROI isn't clear yet, or they can't afford it.

**Response:**
```
"I hear you. Can I ask — is it that the price doesn't fit 
the budget right now, or is it more that you're not sure 
it'll produce enough return to justify it?"
```

If it's ROI:
```
"Let's revisit the math. If we get you [N] clients at 
[contract value] over the next [timeframe], that's [return].
Our fee is [price]. That's a [X]x return. 

Does the math still feel off?"
```

If it's budget:
```
"Understood. Two options: we could start with a smaller scope 
at [lower price], or if timing is the issue, we could start 
[month] when budget opens up. Which fits better?"
```

**Never discount the original offer.** Offer a smaller scope at a lower price instead.

---

## "I need to think about it"

**What they usually mean:** They're not sold yet. There's an unspoken objection.

**Response:**
```
"Of course — this is a real decision. 

Can I ask: is there a specific part you're unsure about, 
or is it more of a gut feeling that you need to sit with?"
```

Let them name it. Then address that specific thing.

If they can't name it:
```
"Totally fine. What would need to be true for this to be 
a clear yes for you?"
```

---

## "I need to talk to my partner / CFO / board"

**Response:**
```
"That makes sense. Who else is involved in this decision?

Would it help if I joined a quick call with them — 
so I can answer their questions directly instead of 
you having to relay everything?"
```

If they prefer to handle it themselves:
```
"Understood. I'll send you a one-pager with the key numbers 
so they have exactly what they need to evaluate it. 
What's the best email for them?"
```

---

## "We've tried cold email before and it didn't work"

**Response:**
```
"That's actually really common — and usually it comes down 
to one of three things: infrastructure issues that sent 
everything to spam, copy that wasn't specific enough, 
or targeting the wrong ICP.

Can I ask what you tried specifically? I want to understand 
what didn't work before telling you we'd get a different result."
```

After they explain:
```
"Got it. Here's what's different about how we'd do it: 
[specific difference that addresses their failure mode]."
```

---

## "I don't have time to manage this"

**Response:**
```
"That's exactly why we built it this way — your involvement 
is [X hours/week] at most: a quick review of the prospect list 
on Monday and approving content before it goes out.

Everything else — research, writing, sending, follow-up — 
we handle. Would [X hours/week] be manageable?"
```

---

## "Can you guarantee results?"

**Response:**
```
"Here's what I can guarantee: [specific deliverable — e.g., 
'we will send X emails to verified prospects with personalized 
openers every month'].

What I can't guarantee is your close rate on calls — 
because that depends on your offer and sales conversation, 
which I don't control.

What I can tell you is that for [N] similar clients, 
the average outcome was [result range]. Does that 
give you enough to move forward?"
```

**If you have a performance guarantee:**
```
"We do offer a [30-day money-back / results-based] guarantee: 
if we don't hit [metric] in [timeframe], [what you do].

That removes the main risk on your side."
```

---

## "I want to wait until [Q2 / after the holidays / new year]"

**Response:**
```
"What's the driver on the timing — is it budget cycle, 
or something else happening in the business?"
```

If it's budget:
```
"Understood. I have one spot opening up in [month] — 
would it make sense to lock it in now and start then?"
```

If it's genuine priority conflict:
```
"Makes sense. When do you want me to follow up? 
I'll put it in my calendar and reach out then — 
no pressure between now and that date."
```

---

## "I found someone cheaper"

**Response:**
```
"That's worth exploring. Can I ask — what's their process 
for personalization? Specifically, how do they make sure 
the first line is based on real recent activity and not 
just a mail merge?"
```

Most cheaper alternatives don't do real personalization. Let them discover that gap themselves.

```
"If they've got a solid system, that might be the right move. 
The reason clients come to us specifically is [differentiator]. 
But if that's not a priority for you, the lower-cost option 
might serve you fine."
```

---

## When to Walk Away

Not every objection deserves more handling. Walk away when:

- They've said no clearly, more than once
- The math doesn't work for them (ROI < 2x at their contract value)
- They want to pay on performance only (no retainer)
- They're looking for a vendor, not a partner
- Every answer reveals a new objection (they're not buying, they're negotiating)

```
"I appreciate the conversation. Based on what you've told me, 
I don't think we're the right fit right now — and I'd rather 
tell you that than take your money and underdeliver.

If anything changes, feel free to reach back out."
```

Walking away cleanly often triggers callbacks from people who were just testing you.
