## Naming criteria

A good newsletter name is:
1. **Memorable** — you can say it once and someone can type it into Google
2. **Specific enough to signal niche** — or so cryptic it becomes a brand (think *Not Boring* — works at scale, risky at 0-1)
3. **Available** — domain + social handles + the URL path on your platform
4. **Readable out loud** — someone recommends it on a podcast, can the listener find it?
5. **Not trademarked** (quick google search + USPTO / EU IPO trademark search)

## Naming approaches

### Approach 1 — Your own name

E.g. *Ben Thompson's Stratechery*, *Lenny's Newsletter*, *Morning Brew by Alex Lieberman*.

- **Pros:** pre-built SEO of your name; can't really be lost; easy to pivot content
- **Cons:** can't sell the asset; feels less "brand-y"; locks you to being the face forever

### Approach 2 — Topic + noun

E.g. *Marketing Examples*, *Product Hunt Daily*, *The Bootstrapped Founder*.

- **Pros:** instant signal of what the newsletter is about
- **Cons:** harder to pivot content; names often taken; SEO competition harder

### Approach 3 — Evocative / metaphorical

E.g. *Not Boring*, *The Hustle*, *Superorganizers*, *Lenny's Newsletter*.

- **Pros:** distinctive; brand-able; pivot-friendly
- **Cons:** requires more marketing to teach people what it's about; harder search discovery

### Approach 4 — "The X" format

E.g. *The Generalist*, *The Information*, *The Pragmatic Engineer*.

- **Pros:** sounds authoritative; clean
- **Cons:** dated + "THE X" names are everywhere; you'll be confused with dozens of others

## The Value Signal Test (Hormozi)

Before committing to a name, run it through this lens:

> **Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood ÷ Time to Result × Effort Required = Perceived Value**

A name that signals a fast, believable outcome outperforms clever or abstract names at the zero-to-one stage.

**Application:** "Freelance OS" (outcome: running your freelance business like a system) scores higher than "The Tuesday Brief" (outcome: unclear) at launch. Abstract names work after brand equity builds — not before.

## 4 Naming Mistakes to Avoid (Dickie Bush)

**Mistake 1: Naming after yourself at launch**
Works for celebrities. Fails at zero subscribers. Your name carries no signal when no one knows you yet. Exception: already well-known in the niche.

**Mistake 2: Going broad**
"Business Weekly" competes with Bloomberg. Broad names have no niche gravity — they attract no one because they signal nothing.

**Mistake 3: Spanning multiple industries**
Trying to cover too many topics in one name confuses the audience. Pick one nucleus. Specificity creates gravity; generality repels it.

**Mistake 4: Clever over clear**
A name that makes you smile but doesn't communicate the outcome fails the Value Signal Test. Clarity beats cleverness before brand equity builds.

## 4-Step Naming Process

1. **List your niche + outcome** — Write your niche in one word and the outcome you deliver in one word. Combine them.
2. **Generate 10 candidates** — Use all 4 naming approaches (above). Aim for variety, not polish.
3. **Run the Value Signal Test** — For each candidate: Is the Dream Outcome clear? Is it believable? Is the signal fast? Is it low-effort to understand?
4. **Check availability** — Domain + Substack URL + social handles + quick trademark search (USPTO / EU IPO).

## 3 Copywriting Tactics for Name Generation

**The "Don't Worry" Whisper:** Address the reader's biggest fear in the name itself. E.g. "No Fluff Startup" removes the fear of wasted time.

**More Problems Solved:** The name signals it solves more than one pain. E.g. "Freelance OS" implies a whole operating system, not just one tip.

**Numbers:** Specificity adds credibility. "5-Minute Marketing" is more believable than "Quick Marketing Tips."
