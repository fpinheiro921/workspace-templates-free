# Newsletter Description

The "mini sales pitch" that sits on your landing page, in your Substack bio, in every social profile. Gets written AFTER niche + name.

## The 3 variants (write all 3)

### Variant 1 — 1 paragraph (50-80 words)

The default. Landing page subhead, pitch email, Twitter bio link context.

Structure:
1. WHO it's for (specific, from `niche-framework.md`)
2. WHAT they'll get (1-2 specific outcomes)
3. HOW often (cadence)
4. (Optional) WHY you — your information advantage in 1 phrase

**Draft:**

<e.g. "A weekly newsletter for solo SaaS founders at $10k-100k MRR who want to monetize LinkedIn without hiring a ghostwriter. Every Thursday, you'll get one tactical play — content frameworks, outreach scripts, offer tweaks — you can test by Monday. Written by someone who built a $30k/mo LinkedIn-driven agency before starting this newsletter.">

### Variant 2 — 3 bullets

Landing page hero, pricing page, about section. Skimmable.

**Draft:**

For <who>, you'll get:
- <outcome 1 — specific>
- <outcome 2 — specific>
- <outcome 3 — specific>

**Example:**
For solo SaaS founders at $10k-100k MRR, you'll get:
- One LinkedIn content framework per week (so you never stare at a blank page)
- Monthly teardown of a $100k+ one-person SaaS operation
- Templates + swipe files drawn from 6 years of ghostwriting

### Variant 3 — 50-word micro-pitch

Twitter bio, Instagram bio, footer of other people's newsletters if they shout you out.

50 words, max. Punchy. No hedge words.

**Draft:**

<e.g. "Weekly newsletter for solo SaaS founders, $10k-100k MRR. One tactical play per Thursday: content frameworks, outreach scripts, offer tweaks you can test by Monday. Written by a former agency owner who's done this for 40+ clients.">

## Writing rules

- **Tangible outcomes** — "get more leads" is vague; "get 3-5 warm inbound replies per week" is tangible (see `_PATTERNS/hook-library-core.md`)
- **Specific audience** — not "entrepreneurs", say who exactly (see `POSITIONING/niche-framework.md`)
- **No banned words** from `CONTEXT/voice-guide.md`
- **No hedging** — "might help you" → remove it or commit
- **Run all 3 variants past the Two-Pass Review** (`_PATTERNS/two-pass-review.md`) before publishing

## Where these go

| Variant | Placement |
|---|---|
| 1-paragraph | Landing page subhead, About page, pitch emails, Substack "About" |
| 3-bullet | Landing page hero section, Pricing page headline support |
| 50-word | Twitter/LinkedIn bio link context, Instagram bio, about-author blocks |

## Update triggers

Rewrite these when:
- Niche sharpens (via `niche-framework.md` update)
- Cadence changes (going from weekly → twice-weekly)
- You add a paid tier
- Subscriber count crosses a 10x (e.g. 1k → 10k) — you have more proof to lean on now

## Category Copywriting Framework

Two frameworks for stress-testing any positioning copy before publishing. Apply to all 3 description variants above.

### Framework 1 — Tangible vs Intangible Language

**Intangible language** sounds big and meaningful but can't be held, felt, or measured. Every reader interprets it differently, so it resonates with no one specifically.

| Intangible | Tangible rewrite |
|-----------|-----------------|
| "I will help you live the life of your dreams." | "I will help you retire with $1M in the bank by age 50." |
| "I will teach you how to be a miraculous writer." | "I will teach you how to write a mystery novel that can sell 10,000+ copies." |
| "I will show you how to save money like a pro." | "I will show you how to save an extra $500 per month." |
| "I write about how to find your dream partner." | "I write about how to find a better second husband after a painful divorce." |

**20 ways to make language tangible** (pick 2–3 and apply to your description draft):

1. Use numbers or dollar figures — "save $300/month" not "save money"
2. Specify timeframes — "double income in 6 months" not "achieve success faster"
3. Define the end result — "lose 15 pounds in 12 weeks" not "get healthier"
4. Mention tools or deliverables — "a 30-day planner" not "resources"
5. Include real-world scenarios — "stay calm in a job interview" not "manage stress"
6. Focus on actions, not states — "run a 5K in 30 min" not "reach fitness goals"
7. Call out specific groups — "first-generation college students" not "young people"
8. Quantify effort — "launch in 7 days" not "get started fast"
9. Break into steps — "in 3 steps" makes it feel achievable
10. Name specific skills — "craft compelling character arcs" not "become a better writer"
11. Use comparison or benchmarks — "close 20% more deals than last quarter"
12. Provide metrics — "7% annual return" not "invest wisely"
13. Describe physical results — "fit into your favorite jeans again"
14. Use specific locations or settings — "stage fright in front of a live audience" not "fear"
15. Highlight tangible deliverables — "a 10-page business plan template"
16. Use exact measurements — "3-layer chocolate cake in under 2 hours"
17. Describe before and after — "cluttered home office → clean workspace in 1 weekend"
18. Use brand names or specific products — grounds abstract claims in reality
19. Define frequency — "10 minutes every morning" not "meditate more"
20. Tie to a specific goal — "save $5,000 for a down payment in 1 year"

### Framework 2 — Objective vs Subjective Language

After making language tangible, stress-test it: ask 10 people to interpret your sentence. If all 10 give the same answer, it's objective. If answers vary, tighten it further.

> "I will help you retire with $1M in the bank by age 50."
> You either retire with $1M by age 50 or you don't. No ambiguity.

**The objection:** "But I can't guarantee those results."
You aren't guaranteeing them. You're promising to provide the frameworks that make them achievable. Your responsibility is delivering the information you promised — the reader's outcome is their responsibility.

Run every description variant through both frameworks before publishing. Tangible + Objective = maximum resonance with the exact right reader.
