# Hook Library — Core

## What it is

The atomic unit of writing online: the hook. This file covers the 6-piece irresistible-headline formula (for long-form titles + newsletter subject lines) and the 6 universal hook types (for social posts + email openers).

## The pattern

### Part A — The 6-piece irresistible headline

Every high-converting long-form headline has up to 6 components, in this order:

1. **HOW MANY** — a number, usually 2-19. Odd numbers outperform even.
2. **THE WHAT** — the topic (Copywriting, Productivity, Investing)
3. **THE HOW** — the proven approach (see 30 "nouns" below)
4. **THE WHO** — the audience (beginners, SaaS founders, busy parents)
5. **THE WHY** — the primary outcome the audience wants
6. **TWIST THE KNIFE** — 1-3 more outcomes stacked

**Example:**
> **7 Copywriting Tips for Beginners to Sell Your First $100 Digital Product, Start Making Money Online, and Eventually Quit Your 9-to-5**

- HOW MANY: 7
- THE WHAT: Copywriting
- THE HOW: Tips
- THE WHO: Beginners
- THE WHY: Sell your first $100 digital product
- TWIST THE KNIFE: Start making money online, quit your 9-to-5

**Tangible vs intangible rule:**
Always tangible. Intangible: "so you can be happier." Tangible: "so you can wake up feeling energized without hitting snooze."

### Part B — 30 "HOW" nouns (the proven approaches)

Pick one to structure any listicle or long-form piece:

Tips / Skills / Tools / Traits / Steps / Goals / Books / Habits / Stories / Quotes / Secrets / Insights / Benefits / Lessons / Reasons / Creators / Routines / Mistakes / Podcasts / Examples / Questions / Inventions / Templates / Resources / Challenges / Companies / Data Points / Realizations / Frameworks / Presentations.

### Part C — The 6 universal hook types (for opening lines)

Apply to: tweet openers, LinkedIn first 200 characters, newsletter first sentence, email subject lines, YouTube titles.

1. **Strong declarative statement** — `Ghostwriting is the highest-ROI skill of the decade.`
2. **Thought-provoking question** — `What if your niche is wider than you think?`
3. **Controversial opinion** — `Writing books is the worst ghostwriting service.`
4. **Vulnerable statement** — `I spent 3 years chasing the wrong clients.`
5. **Weird / unique insight, stat, or data point** — `1,500 ghostwriters in PGA. Zero started with an audience.`
6. **Meaningful moment-in-time** — `In 2019, I was $40k in debt. Today I run a 7-figure agency.`

### Part D — Subject-line compression (email-specific)

When compressing a long headline into an email subject line:
- **30-50 characters** / **4-8 words**
- **Sentence case**: capitalize first word only, rest lower-case
- If starting with a number, keep the next word lowercase too
- Drop everything except HOW MANY + THE HOW + THE WHAT + a tight WHY

**Examples:**
- Long: `7 Copywriting Tips for Beginners to Sell Your First $100 Digital Product…`
- Short: `7 copywriting tips for beginners.` ← 33 characters
- Long: `3 Ways to Structure Your Next Ghostwriting Project and Get Paid a Premium`
- Short: `3 ways to land your first client.` ← 33 characters

Always present **5 subject-line variants** when generating. User picks.

- Context: the 6 hook types + tangible vs intangible rule + examples
- Final Delivery: "Present 10 variants in a bulleted list, labeled by hook type."

### In a landing page (saas-micro, fullstack-writer)

Hero headline = HOW MANY + THE WHAT + THE HOW + THE WHO + THE WHY (skip TWIST THE KNIFE — too long for hero).

### In social content

Use Part C hook types as the first 200 characters of any LinkedIn post or first sentence of any tweet. Must end on a cliffhanger — reader should want to click "See more" or scroll down.

## Part E — The 4-Part Hook Formula (Erica Schneider)

Every strong hook has up to 4 components. Not all are required, but the best hooks have at least 2:

1. **Specificity** — a concrete detail that makes it feel real, not generic. Numbers, names, timeframes, dollar amounts.
2. **Relatability** — the reader sees themselves in the situation. Mirror their exact language back at them.
3. **Emotional resonance** — connects to a fear, desire, frustration, or aspiration they already carry.
4. **Curiosity gap** — opens a loop they can only close by reading on. A gap between what they know and what you're about to tell them.

**Diagnosis test for a weak hook:** Which of the 4 is missing? That's your fix.
- No specificity → add a number, name, or concrete detail
- No relatability → rewrite in the reader's words, not yours
- No emotional resonance → name the feeling they're already having
- No curiosity gap → withhold the answer until the body

## Part F — Hook Power Triangle (Rob Lennon)

Three types of hooks that dominate high-performing content. The best hooks use all three angles at once — most only use one:

**1. Novelty** — ideas the reader hasn't encountered before
- Counter-intuitive (proves a common belief wrong)
- Counter-narrative (challenges the accepted story)
- Shock & awe (surprising stat, fact, or outcome)
- Elegant articulation (says perfectly what everyone thinks but nobody has said)

**2. Resonance** — ideas that land emotionally
- Clearly understood via simple, short sentences + examples + counterexamples
- Persuasive reasoning (logical, not just emotional)
- Open loops (sustained curiosity — promise is made but not yet delivered)
- Story, analogy, and metaphor (activates imagination, not just logic)

**3. Style** — ideas that are a pleasure to read
- Vivid and specific — engages imagination, not abstraction
- Occasional eccentricity or humor
- Plain phrases replaced with vivid metaphors
- Unimportant details removed ruthlessly

**Test:** Does your hook use at least one element from each corner of the triangle?

## Part G — Story Starters (3 Frameworks)

For hooks that open with a story rather than a claim or question:

**Framework 1 — The Before Moment**
Drop the reader into a specific moment *before* a change happened. No setup. No context. Mid-scene.
> *"It was 11pm. I had $11 in my account and a client threatening to cancel."*

**Framework 2 — The Contrast Cut**
Open with what was true, then cut immediately to what's true now. Let the gap do the work.
> *"Three years ago I was writing $50 blog posts. Last Tuesday I closed a $25,000 contract."*

**Framework 3 — The Stranger in the Room**
Open with an observation that shouldn't exist — something surprising, contradictory, or out of place.
> *"The worst-written post in my feed had 4,000 likes. The most polished had 12."*

**Rules for story hooks:**
- First sentence is always past tense and mid-action
- No scene-setting before the tension
- Specific sensory detail beats vague emotion every time

## Part H — 10 Content Formats and Their Hook Logic

Different formats require different hook strategies. Match format to hook type:

| Format | Default Hook Type | Key Principle |
|---|---|---|
| **Tweet / short post** | Strong declarative OR curiosity gap | One sentence. Must stop the scroll alone. |
| **Thread / carousel** | Novelty + outcome promise | Hook = promise of the whole thread's value |
| **Newsletter** | Resonance + open loop | Hook is the first sentence of the email body, not the subject line |
| **Blog post / article** | 6-piece headline formula (Part A) | Hook is the headline — body hook is secondary |
| **LinkedIn long-form** | Vulnerable or controversial + cliffhanger | First 2 lines visible before "See more" — must earn the click |
| **YouTube title** | Curiosity gap + specificity | Numbers + tension + outcome |
| **Email subject line** | Subject-line compression (Part D) | 40–60 chars, one angle only |
| **Sales page headline** | Outcome + identity | State the transformation, not the features |
| **Course lesson hook** | Stakes + outcome (Part E) | "After this, you'll be able to…" then immediately why that matters |
| **Podcast / video intro** | Story Starter (Part G) | Mid-scene drop-in, establish tension in 10 seconds |

## Part I — Erica Schneider's 7 Hook Templates

Fill-in-the-blank frameworks for Twitter/X and LinkedIn hooks. Start here when you have a topic but no hook angle.

**Template 1 — Proof + Pain + Outcome**
> I've [quantifiable proof]. But [pain point]. How to [outcome]:

**Template 2 — State of Play + Pain + Reframe**
> [State of play]. But [pain point]. Why (and what to do instead):

**Template 3 — Opinion + Bad Take + Social Proof + Playbook**
> [Opinion]. Stance on [bad take]. I've spent [social proof + timeframe] to achieve [outcome]. The playbook:

**Template 4 — Hard Thing + Proof + Solution**
> [X] is hard. [Data + proof]. Do [Y] to get [outcome]:

**Template 5 — Shared Pain + Personal History + Overcome**
> Everybody [pain point]. I used to [pain point], too. Here's what I've done to [overcome and get new outcome]:

**Template 6 — Difficult Situation + Desire + Victory**
> [Specific difficult situation]. [Desire]. What I did to overcome [pain point] and achieve [outcome]:

**Template 7 — Social Proof + Learning + Outcome**
> I've spent [social proof] learning about [concept]. [Details.] Here are my [X learnings] to help you [outcome]:

**Rules for all 7 templates:**
- Every template requires a **quantifiable or specific detail** — never leave the proof vague
- The pain point should mirror the reader's exact language back at them
- The outcome must be tangible (see Part A, tangible vs intangible rule)
- These are frameworks, not boxes — rework and expand as needed

**Anti-patterns — never use these hook styles:**
- ❌ "It should be illegal" — cheap shock, no credibility
- ❌ "Teach you more than a college degree" — false promise, reader distrusts immediately
- ❌ "More in 2 minutes than you learned in X years" — same problem
- ❌ "Outperform 99% of people" — unverifiable, reader-hostile
- ❌ "Make you millions" — triggers spam filters, destroys trust
- ❌ Any hook that over-promises an outcome you can't actually deliver

## Part J — 5 Script Archetypes (Short-Form Video)

Different video scripts serve different functions. Choose based on goal, not habit.

| Type | Best for | Length | Structure |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Affirmation/Vision** | Emotional connection, high save rate | 45–90 sec | Vision → Sensory details → Affirmation close |
| **Problem-Solution** | Demonstrating authority, providing value | 45–75 sec | Pain → Understanding → Reframe → Relief |
| **Quiet Truth** | Deep connection, "I needed this" comments | 60–90 sec | Soft hook → Gentle truth → Sitting with it → Quiet close |
| **Pattern Interrupt** | Reaching new audiences, algorithm boost | 30–60 sec | Unexpected hook → Quick pivot → Value → Close |
| **Story-Based** | Memorable content, shares | 60–90 sec | Story setup → Story moment → Lesson → Application |

**Quick selector:**

| Goal | Type | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Build emotional bond | Affirmation/Vision | Heavy on hope, light on teaching |
| Overcome skepticism | Problem-Solution | Pair with Testimonial Bridge CTA |
| Reach new people | Pattern Interrupt | Hook must stop cold traffic |
| Deep resonance | Quiet Truth | Feels like advice from a wise friend |
| Shareable content | Story-Based | Metaphor or mini-story as vehicle |

## Part K — 3 Conversion Levels

For any script that includes a CTA, choose a level based on audience warmth.

### Level 1 — Soft Bridge
- **Ratio:** 80% value, 20% CTA
- **Feels like:** "Here's something helpful… and if you want more, here's where to go"
- **Use when:** Regular content, warming cold audiences, nurturing
- **Structure:** Hook → Value (genuine insight they can use) → Bridge ("and if you want the complete system…") → Soft CTA (invitation to next step)

### Level 2 — Testimonial Bridge
- **Ratio:** 60% proof, 40% invitation
- **Feels like:** "Look what happened for them… you could be next"
- **Use when:** You have real results to share; audience needs proof before acting
- **Structure:** Hook (specific result) → Story (brief testimonial with concrete details) → Connection ("You might be feeling the same way they were…") → CTA ("Here's how to start / get the same result")

### Level 3 — Direct Ask
- **Ratio:** 30% context, 70% offer
- **Feels like:** "Here's what I have, here's what it does, here's how to get it"
- **Use when:** Launches, limited-time offers, warm audience ready to buy
- **Structure:** Hook (direct about the offer) → What it is (clear specific description) → What they get (benefits not features) → Proof (why believe this works) → Why now (urgency/scarcity only if real) → CTA (exactly what to do, one action)

**CTA mechanisms by level:**

| Level | CTA Mechanism |
|---|---|
| 1 | Soft Bridge CTA ("If you want more of this…") or Implied Consequence |
| 2 | Testimonial Bridge CTA ("[Name] was where you are 3 months ago. Now [result].") |
| 3 | Direct Ask ("Link in bio. Join us.") or DM Trigger ("DM me [WORD]") |

## Part L — Urgency and Scarcity Rules

**Real urgency only.** Fake urgency destroys trust permanently.

### Allowed — real urgency
- Limited spots when there's an actual capacity limit
- Enrollment closing with a real date (cohort-based)
- Bonus expiring when you're actually removing it
- Price increasing when you're actually raising it

### Never use — fake scarcity
- "Only 10 spots left!" when unlimited
- "Price doubles tomorrow!" when it doesn't
- "Last chance ever!" when you'll offer it again

### Real urgency language examples
> "I'm opening 50 spots this week. Last time they filled in 3 days."

> "Enrollment closes Friday because we start together Monday."

> "The bonus call is only for people who join before [date]."

Level 3 scripts without real scarcity should use Implied Consequence CTA instead of fake deadline pressure.
