## The 5 Revenue Lanes

Pick 1-3 to start. Full-stack writers eventually run 4-5 in parallel.

### Lane 1 — Affiliate

Recommend products/services you actually use; earn a cut of each sale through the partner's link.

- **Pros:** zero product creation, works with a small audience, starts earning Day 1
- **Cons:** commission rates vary (5-50%), risk of diluting trust if you push products you don't believe in
- **Best fit:** newsletters with niche-loyal audiences
- **Stage:** can run at any stage, including 0-1

### Lane 2 — Sponsorship

A brand pays you to feature their product in your content (newsletter, podcast, video).

- **Pros:** high ticket ($500-$5k+ per placement at modest audience sizes)
- **Cons:** requires ~5k engaged subscribers minimum; ad ops overhead; can feel gross
- **Best fit:** mid-to-high-traffic newsletters, podcasts, YouTube
- **Stage:** typically 1k-10k+ subscribers

### Lane 3 — Subscription / Paid Tier

Part of your content is free; premium subscribers get more (deeper posts, archive access, community, live calls).

- **Pros:** recurring revenue, deep audience relationship, predictable
- **Cons:** requires ~1-2% conversion from free → paid; high content quality bar
- **Best fit:** writers with a strong voice + niche expertise
- **Stage:** 1k+ free subscribers usually needed

### Lane 4 — Digital Product

One-time purchases: ebooks, courses, templates, tools, notion systems, prompt libraries.

- **Pros:** highest profit margin, asset that sells forever, scales without you
- **Cons:** requires a product launch (marketing work), customer support, updates over time
- **Best fit:** creators with taught-expertise in a niche
- **Stage:** any, but easier after 1k+ subscribers

### Lane 5 — Services / Coaching

Trading time for money: ghostwriting, consulting, coaching, done-for-you implementation.

- **Pros:** fastest to first revenue, highest hourly rate at skill ceiling, builds expertise
- **Cons:** doesn't scale without hiring; income caps at your time
- **Best fit:** early-stage writers, specialists, anyone building a service business
- **Stage:** 0-1 and beyond — often the first lane opened

## The Offer Stack Framework

Use this to package any service or product so it's easy to sell and easy to price.

### The 10 variables of a complete offer

1. **Irresistible Offer** — what problem? what cause? what happens if unsolved? → what's the solution? what benefits? what's the dream outcome?
2. **Productized Offer Stack** — the deliverables list (5-15 specific things they get)
3. **Project Timeline** — first dream outcome achieved over what horizon (6 months is the sweet spot for ongoing services)
4. **Price Anchor** — what does NOT solving this cost them (time / money / opportunity)? OR what's the dream outcome worth to them?
5. **Project Cost or Monthly Cost** — asset = flat project price; ongoing service = (project price) ÷ months
6. **Offer Bundling Discount** — bundle 2+ services, give 10-20% off the sum
7. **Pre-Payment Discount** — 10-20% off for paying full project up-front
8. **Package Name** — punchy, outcome-oriented ("Newsletter Autopilot", "LinkedIn Acceleration")
9. **Two Packages** — show two options; second ≈ 2x volume for ~1.7x price (slight volume discount)
10. **Deliverable Descriptions** — each of the 5-15 deliverables paired with 1-4 sentences on why it matters

### 6-month engagement framing

For ongoing services, don't sell "monthly retainers." Sell a **6-month outcome** that happens to be worked on monthly.

> "In 6 months from now, you'll have: a library of 24 high-performing posts, a growing email list, and a repeatable content engine. Here's how we get there, month by month…"

This reframes the buying decision from "do I want to pay $X this month?" to "do I want that outcome in 6 months?"

### Price anchor template

> "To hire someone full-time with [skill 1], [skill 2], and [skill 3] would cost you $100k/year ($8-10k/mo) — and you'd need to find one person who has all three. Or you do it yourself and it eats 15+ hours/week. Or you hire me, and I handle it. $X/mo."

## How to apply

### Picking lanes at your stage

| Stage | Recommended lanes |
|---|---|
| 0-1 (no audience yet) | Services primarily. Affiliate as a side-lane. |
| 1-10k subs | Services + Digital Product + Affiliate |
| 10k-100k | Services (higher ticket) + Digital Product + Sponsorship |
| 100k+ | Subscription + Digital Product + Sponsorship + Services (selective) |

### Stacking lanes deliberately

Don't run 5 lanes poorly. Run 2-3 well. Each lane you add has overhead (tax complexity, audience messaging, customer support, delivery ops). Add the next lane only when the current ones are operating without your daily input.

### In a template

Each lane file (`MONETIZATION/affiliate.md`, etc.) should cover:
- **When this lane makes sense** (stage gate)
- **Economics** (typical CPA, revshare %, retention)
- **How to set it up** (platform, contracts, tracking)
- **Promotion cadence** (how often to mention, where)
- **Metrics to watch**
- **Common traps**
